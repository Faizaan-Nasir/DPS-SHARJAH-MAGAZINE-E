 "The other line moves faster"





This is exactly what goes through my head when I am in a shopping line. I am someone who understands shopping but does not at the same time.

Shopping is an activity in which a customer browses the available goods or services presented by one or more retailers with the potential intent to purchase a suitable selection of them. Wait, wha-? So basically, shopping is buying something from shops. We mostly buy groceries and other necessary items. Stress on the word mostl

I would like to share a quote that I think all of us can relate to. The odds of going to the store for a loaf of bread and coming out with only a loaf of bread are three billion to one. We buy a lot of things that we really don’t need. Here are reasons why:

  1. Class that follows the current trends and is up to date with everything. Even if this means following a stupid trend that involves dumping a bucket of cold water on your head or buying a watermelon and 2000 rubber-bands just to see if entwining them around the watermelon will make it explode.
  

  2. Then, there are some people who are just simply addicted to shopping. Shopping runs through their veins.

 

  3. Some people shop without knowing what they want. Just simply trudge into the mall, breathe in the AC, hear kids crying over desires, watch people stuff their faces at the food court, and get into the line for the one dirham McDonald’s ice cream just to find that the machine doesn’t freaking work. 

  4. Sometimes, you just shop because your house doesn’t have air conditioning, but the mall does.

 

     All these spendings are amplified by shopping festivals, such as DSF. DSF stands for Dubai Shopping Festival. During this, the prices of everything are reduced. That’s great, you might say, and I totally agree but the catch is that we aren’t seeking a particular thing. We are just buying stuff for the sake of buying stuff. Doing that leaves you with a house full of totally useless stuff (like lightsabers, Pikachu stuffed figures) and a long credit card bill.

When you think expensive, think duty-free. Basically, there’s no tax. We see most of duty-free at airports. I was casually looking at the shops (because my parents made us come to the airport 6 hours before our flight took off, just to be on the safe side) and my eyes go to the chocolate shop (my soul is still that of a 5 year old) I look at the price of a normal Toblerone. I rub my eyes and look at the price again. It's 40 AED for 6 Toblerones! If I go to buy the same deal somewhere else, I will get it for 20 dirhams. They also sell watches priced at 90,000 dirhams, but that’s a different matter.

Global Village. With the excuse of wanting to know more about other countries’ cultures, we just straight up go on a shopping spree. Oh, that keychain signifies the struggle and culture of the Africans, let’s buy it as a reminder that I wasted money once again! Let’s buy a magnet of this small town in Yugoslavia. Let’s buy this sound making drum thingy for my uncle’s nephew’s brother. Global Village does have a great concept, but many people end up buying more materialistic things rather than gaining knowledge.

Sometimes we get so immersed in shopping that some parents forget their kids in shops or the kid themselves get lost. I have had a similar experience when I was smaller. You know how attractive toys are. I was so absorbed in the toys that I completely lost track of where my parents were. And I got lost. As a 6-year-old, you know what my emotional reaction would have been. I cried and cried. After a minute of wandering with no purpose, there they were. Right behind the aisles, looking at fancy bread. (why was the bread aisle next to the toy aisle? I want to talk to your manager)

If we go to buy 2 things, we bring back another 20 things. A reason for this is advertising. Every ad gives the same message: your life will be better if you buy this product. We hear this so many times that we sort of start believing it.

There are 4 ways they make us buy products:

One way that they make us buy products are nice logos like the one Amazon has. Come on, can you resist a box smiling at you?
The second way is that the products target insecurities like weight loss, hair restoration, diets, etc.
The third method is using attractive colors. Products that are on sale have names emblazoned with red letters and signs.
The fourth and last method is by planning. That’s why the floorplan of stores looks like a maze. They are designed for you to look at every product. An excellent example of this is Ikea. I’ve gone to Ikea to buy a dustbin. We came out with a dustbin, a pot, some flowers, and chairs.

We go out to refresh ourselves. But we come back with an exhausted body and exhausted wallet. I get so exhausted from shopping that I don’t even freshen up. Crash onto the bed in those jeans and toes hurting from the Skechers that said memory foam does wonders.

I need to go, my mom’s asking me where the money went.
